Overall Analysis Questions

When starting this assignment, I decided to begin by framing my work into three "big questions" and with subcategories that I could delve further into. These questions were written without any knowledge of what the dataset looked like, which is something that I might change about my process in the future. These three big questions were:

  1. Who is getting evicted?
  2. Who is doing the evictions?
  3. Where are evictions happening?

For my first question, I wanted to look more specifically into the range and number of household incomes, the racial demographic breakdowns, and the age ranges represented in eviction data. Readings in class such as "Housing Justice Beyond the Emergency" have established various communities that are the most vulnerable to evictions, and they include single mothers, communities of color, and younger families. I want to look at these trends quanitatively from an intersectional lens, and try to examine how overlapping identites may affect their evictions. I believe that understanding who is most affected by this issue will be a great starting point to creating more impactful visualizations.

For my second question, I want to look more specifically into the prevelance of corporate landlords, learn what kinds of businesses most often take part in this practice, and find out how long they've been in this market. I think that this line of questions will help me understand the extent of influence that corporate landlords have over the housing sector and how the companies in this business interact with each other. This information is especially important to me because the idea of private equity groups acting as landlords is a very new concept to me. I first learned about it through the readings for A1, "When Private Equity Becomes Your Landlord." The stories of the folks displaced in this article concerned me a lot, and were a large part of the reason why I chose this subtheme to study.

For my third question, I wanted to approach this subtheme with a more spatial lens, exploring how community location affects eviction rates and the prevalence of corporate landlords. I hope to be able to overlay eviction location information or corporate landlord presence with any spatial data that exists on the information in Question 1, including racial, age, and socioeconomic demographics. I believe that spatial data will provide insights that graphs alone won't be able to provide; in my urban planning classes, we are constantly reminded of how important space and place are when disecting issues like evictions, especially when spatial forces such as redlining have been used to keep communities of colors from accessing housing stability.

Some of the questions that I was hoping to explore were infeasible with the corporate landlords dataset, such as the trends in racial breakdowns of renters for each neighborhood over time and any spatial information. To be honest, I struggled quite a bit with this assignment. I found it a bit difficult to create visualizations with the corporate landlord data provided, because of its limited set of dimensions and measures. I saw that more eviction data will be provided as this class progresses, and I'm excited to analyze it when it comes! Still, I hope to keep my guiding questions above in mind as I progress onto A3 and the final project.

Summary

In this dataset, we were provided a variety of statistics for a neighborhood, as well as the corporate ownership and owner occupancy rates over the years of 2004 to 2024. With this data, I was able to visualize corporate ownership and owner occupancy trends over time and their correlation to various groups provided in the dataset. I was able to find that corporate ownership is increasing across boston, and is negatively correlated with owner occupancy, positively correlated with unit vacancy, and positively correlated with group quartering rates. Additionally, I found that while owner occupancy is decreasing on average in Boston, each neighborhood is experiencing very different trends in owner occupancy. In this vein, I also found that the change in owner occupancy is correlated with how non-white a community is. Finally, I found that communities are potentially becoming more diverse (or less white) and that there are various neighborhood groups that may serve as great case studies for future work in this subtheme.
